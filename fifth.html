Plasma Screen

What sets the Portable III apart from its predecesors is the amber gas plasma display. The plasma display made it possible to drop a few pounds and be smaller than if it were to use a small CRT like the Portables that came becore it. Plasma displays of this era, however are infamous for being delicate and hard to repair. The Column Driver ICs that power each line drawn on the screen are sensitive to dust, causing shorts. This is what causes pale orange lines and/or dead spots to appear on the screen. Dead spots can also appear for another reason, worn out solder. The high voltage nature of this part causes it to run hot, and the constant heating and cooling from either normal use or being exposed to heat and cold puts a strain on the solder joints of the column drivers. Dead spots indicate circuits that are failing to transport power at all. Bad electrolytic capacitors can also cause a "fog" to appear on screen. Be very careful when working on this part, the flex and ribbon cables are very delicate in addition to high voltage. When cleaning this part, the technician's manual states to ONLY use isopropyl alcohol when cleaning the inside of the screen, in addition it is also reccommended by hobbyists to use the strongest alcohol you can find when doing so.

The column driver ICs are the biggest points of failure, each leg of this chip controls one horizontal or vertical line of pixels on the screen, in total they make a resolution of 640x400 pixels. Note that the horizontal column drivers are located underneath side panels, making them tricky to access.

There are two hardware revisions to the screen. Revision 1 (left) is typically found on 286 models while Revision 2 (right) is found on 386 models. These are both interchangable with each other which makes the repair option of using oldstock or a donor unit (a unit that is beyond repair but had a working part that can be repurposed for use on a repairable one) relatively easy. However, the amount of donor units and new old stock are becoming increasingly rare and expensive, and the intent of this article is to attempt to repair these screens. Screen Revision 2 may be easier to repair due to its side panels being accessible by screw. Revision one is held in place by plastic buttons which will have to be pried off and replaced if the horizontal drivers need attention. Note that the central PCB is screwed down by Pozidriv screws, so make sure to get the proper screwdiver, as a phillips screwdriver will only strip the screws. After the screws, the PCB is held down by 4 white ribbon cables. Removing these is also tricky because of how little slack they have and the face that oxidation may crudely weld them in place, which deoxit can fix. Though without those problems you should be able to wiggle them out.

These are the only two electrolytic capacitors on Screen Revision 1, as you can see in this case, there was a leak in one leading to corosion on the cap leg and solder joint. A problem like this can lead to a foggy looking screen. Replacement capacitors can be found at Digikey or Mouser. When replacing caps make sure to check the voltage and capcitance of the originals, which is clearly written on the capacitors most of the time. In this case the value of the two caps are as follows:

  1. 16V, 100uf(left)
  2. 250V, 2.2uf(right)

When replacing a capacitor always match the capacitance exactly, but you can go higher in voltage because voltage is how many volts the cap can handle, not what it produces. If you can find capacitors with both correct voltage and capacitance, go with the one that matches original values.
